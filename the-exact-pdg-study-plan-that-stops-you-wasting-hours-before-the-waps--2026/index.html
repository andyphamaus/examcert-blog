The Exact PDG Study Plan That Stops You Wasting Hours Before the WAPS Test

Most airmen don't fail the PFE because they're not smart enough. They fail because they "studied" by reading the PDG cover to cover once, two weeks out, highlighting random sentences, and then walking into the test center hoping muscle memory kicks in. It doesn't. The Promotion Fitness Examination is 80 questions of recall, and recall is a trained skill, not a vibe.

So here's the plan I wish somebody had handed me my first cycle. It assumes you have roughly eight weeks before your test window. If you have less, compress it, don't skip steps. This is built around the PDG (formally Air Force Handbook 1, the Airman handbook) because that book is the single source the PFE is written from. If it's not in AFH 1, it's not on the PFE. Burn that into your head before you waste an evening memorizing a regulation that isn't testable.

First, get the right book and the EPRRC

Before week one, do two things. Download the current AFH 1 (the February 2025 edition is the live one as of this cycle, and the handbook gets revised every couple of years, so confirm you're not studying a 2021 PDF some buddy emailed you). It's on Air Force e-publishing, and a copy lives on your government desktop. Then pull the Enlisted Promotion References and Requirements Catalog (EPRRC) for your grade. The EPRRC tells you exactly which chapters and references are testable for the PFE and the USAFSC (the U.S. Air Force Supervisory Course exam, the one that replaces the SKT-style load for senior promotions). Studying the whole handbook when only specific sections are fair game is the single biggest hour-waster out there.

Quick note on how it all connects: the PDG feeds the PFE directly, and it also feeds the supervisory exam content for higher grades. Same book, different slices, different weighting. Your WAPS score is built from test points plus your other factors, so every PFE question you nail is points you can't get back later. Treat the book like the answer key it actually is. The week-by-week curriculum

Don't read linearly. Read by weight. Here's the rotation that works.

Weeks 1-2 — Heritage, history, and the profession of arms. Chapter 1 in the new edition is Professionalism (they moved it up front and renumbered everything after it, so don't trust old chapter numbers). Pair it with Air Force history and heritage. This material is dense with names, dates, and "first to do X" facts that the PFE loves. Read 10 pages a day, then immediately write down five facts from memory without looking. The writing-it-down part is the whole point.

Weeks 3-4 — Organization and the enlisted force structure. How the Air Force and now the Space Force are organized, the chain, MAJCOMs, the enlisted force development tiers. This is where people get sloppy because it feels like background. It isn't. There are easy, predictable points here if you can recall the structure cleanly.

Weeks 5-6 — Promotions, evaluations, and force management. This is the meat: Chapter 9 territory, promotion systems and programs, the Airman Comprehensive Assessment, performance evaluations, reenlistment and continuation, awards and decorations. If you're going for the supervisory exam, this is also your highest-yield block, so slow down and own it.

Week 7 — Customs, courtesies, standards, and benefits. Lighter reading, lots of discrete facts. Good week to clean up everything you skimmed earlier.

Week 8 — No new reading. Test simulation only. This week you stop "studying" and start testing yourself under conditions that look like the real thing.

How to actually retain it (the part everyone skips)

Reading is input. Testing is retention. The research on this is boring and consistent: pulling a fact out of your brain wires it in far harder than reading the same fact ten times. So after every reading block, quiz yourself. Don't reread, recall.

Three tactics that carry the most weight:

  • Active recall. Close the book, ask yourself the question, struggle to answer, then check. The struggle is what builds the memory.
  • Spaced repetition. Re-quiz week-1 material in week 3, again in week 6. The facts you got right early are exactly the ones you'll forget by test day if you never revisit them.
  • Question-bank practice over rereading. A good bank exposes you to how the PFE phrases things, the qualifiers, the "all of the following except," the date traps. That phrasing is half the battle. Aim to spend at least half your total study time answering questions, not reading. If you flip that ratio you'll know the book and still bomb the test.

The traps that quietly cost points

A few things that sink otherwise prepared airmen:

Studying an outdated PDG. The handbook gets revised, chapters get renumbered, policy references change, Space Force content gets added. A two-year-old PDF will teach you wrong answers. Verify the edition every cycle.

Memorizing things that aren't testable. If the EPRRC didn't flag it, it's not on the PFE. Don't grind regulations that feel important but aren't on the list.

Cramming dates the night before. Dates and "firsts" need spaced repetition over weeks. The night before, you should be reviewing your weak areas and sleeping, not absorbing 40 new facts that won't survive until morning.

Treating the supervisory exam like the PFE. Different grade, different emphasis on leadership and force-management content. Match your study slice to the test you're actually sitting.

Skipping timed practice. The PFE is 80 questions on a clock. If your first timed run is the real thing, you'll burn time second-guessing. Do at least three full timed simulations in week 8.

Make the last two weeks count

By week 7 you should be scoring consistently on practice questions in your weak chapters, not just your strong ones. If a chapter keeps biting you, that's your signal to spend an extra evening there instead of rereading material you already own.
